The 1950s – The Birth of Data

The first great calculating machines spent the 1940s helping the Allied powers win World War II. Just before 1950, many machines whose existence had been classified were unveiled to the public virtually all at once, battle-tested and ready to rescue a 20th century

The first great calculating machines spent the 1940s helping the Allied powers win World War II. Just before 1950, many machines whose existence had been classified were unveiled to the public virtually all at once, battle-tested and ready to rescue a 20th century that, to anyone who survived up to that point, seemed overrated. The world needed to get to work, and the first business computers pointed the way forward.

By 1949, Cambridge University had successfully engineered a way for EDSAC’s memory tubes to retain binary digits, earning it the moniker “electronic brain.” England, America, and a re-emergent Germany raced to develop commercial models that could fund their own research. UNIVAC, first purchased by the US Census Bureau, made evident the need to make workloads easily portable. Magnetic tape provided input and output for the first time, and in so doing, divided program instructions from numeric values. In describing how this system worked, UNIVAC’s engineers re-injected an old Latin word into the world’s vocabulary: data.

1960s

The 1960s – Proof of Concept

Throughout the business world, the 1950s portrayed computers as ‘electronic brains.’ With most business executives still never having touched a computer by 1960, it’s no wonder it was perceived as more of a personality trapped in a metal cabinet, than as a functional business tool.

Throughout the business world, the 1950s portrayed computers as ’electronic brains.’ With most business executives still never having touched a computer by 1960, it’s no wonder it was perceived as more of a personality trapped in a metal cabinet, than as a functional business tool. For computers to be accepted in the everyday workplace, they needed a working demo.

Nothing brings a concept down to earth like a good space program. America’s mission to the moon re-invented the computer. No longer was it a gargantuan box that digested data from tapes and punch cards. NASA redesigned it as a networked communications complex, with operations centers stationed across the planet. At its heart was a data center, which maintained the central repository for its network. Remote terminals, grouped together in command centers, became workstations. The connections between these components were interfaces. The Apollo mission became the staging ground for how interoperable organizations would function throughout the next half-century.

1970s

The 1970s – Building Smaller Things Big

The space program proved that miniaturization not only made for less expensive computers but for better built ones. But none of the major manufacturers in 1970 — Honeywell, Sperry Univac, Burroughs, DEC, and maybe least of all, IBM — directly foresaw the evolutionary path from microchips to miniaturization to mass production to democratization of computing technology.

When Intel produced its 4004 microprocessor in 1971, its intended use was in everyday devices like fire alarm systems and traffic lights. Three years later, when Intel’s 8080 became available on the open market, garage hobbyists were the first to devise 8-bit programming language interpreters and rudimentary operating systems. And when this new “software” was offered to Intel, IBM, and others, they rejected it as having no useful value.

So the hobbyists created an industry unto themselves. By 1977, its leaders were already firmly established: the hobbyist electronics retailer Radio Shack; the former calculator manufacturer Commodore; and a quirky new brand led by a couple of renegades from an oscilloscope maker and a video game company that, in honor of the Beatles, adopted the name Apple Computer. Together, these consumer brands precipitated the decline of the mainframe as the principal facilitator of computing power.

1980s

The 1980s – The Rise of Clones

As the first great era of ubiquitous computing came to an end, the question of how the world would compute was being addressed by hardware: the design of the processor, the memory map of the internal ROM, the speed of storage and retrieval.

As the first great era of ubiquitous computing came to an end, the question of how the world would compute was being addressed by hardware: the design of the processor, the memory map of the internal ROM, the speed of storage and retrieval.

The world of computing as we know it today was largely shaped by the decision made by IBM to let a small company, Microsoft, design and implement the operating system for the IBM PC, called PC DOS, which enabled the small software maker license the same operating system, now called MS-DOS, to others.

IBM may have assumed there would be no “others” that could produce a personal computer that had the scale and interoperability of an IBM machine. The reverse-engineering of IBM’s BIOS, coupled with Microsoft’s ability to license MS-DOS, led to the era of “IBM compatibility.” The rise of “clone” computers led to a computing platform with unprecedented scale and reach into businesses.

Businesses began to see the need to coordinate and synchronize their computing operations. Local-area networking refocused the computing industry away from CPUs, floppy diskettes, and the first hard drives, and toward sharing, interoperability, and bandwidth. Novell, 3Com, and the first microcomputer network engineers hammered out the first communications principles for the new medium.

It was the software industry that drove the course of computing for the first time – and from that time forward. The first file-sharing operating systems and the arrival of Ethernet hardware addressed the task of reconciling transactions from multiple users in the same office.

1990s

The 1990s – Bringing the World Closer

The Internet was a reality as early as the late 1970s, but back then it was seen as little more than an academic experiment. By the dawn of the 1980s, a few thousand people began using it as an exchange mechanism for electronic mail.

The Internet was a reality as early as the late 1970s, but back then it was seen as little more than an academic experiment. By the dawn of the 1980s, a few thousand people began using it as an exchange mechanism for electronic mail. What would set off a global economic revolution was, ironically, another academic experiment: a curiosity, being investigated by a CERN particle physicist in his spare time, about making a HyperCard-like document stack supported by a networked database rather than a localized one.

Tim Berners-Lee’s little experiment first tripped off an electronic publishing revolution. But it was dwarfed by an even greater one: the discovery that programs could be carried out by networks rather than just the computers connected by networks. The World-Wide Web completed the computer’s transformation from a calculating machine, to a communications device, to a social tool. It became the fuel for developing nations, educating and empowering people hundreds of miles distant from anything resembling a school, making millions of people into full participants in the world.

2000s

The 2000s – The Advent of Distributed Computing

The “dot-com bubble” was the biggest and hardest lesson ever learned by the computing industry. Following the terrorist attacks of 2001, the fact that so many millions of consumers were interested in modem communications and the early Web, was no longer enough for investors to place big bets on startups without business models.

The “dot-com bubble” was the biggest and hardest lesson ever learned by the computing industry. Following the terrorist attacks of 2001, the fact that so many millions of consumers were interested in modem communications and the early Web, was no longer enough for investors to place big bets on startups without business models.

It no longer made sense for anyone to build a new computing technology without a very compelling reason. Cost reduction became the foundation for essentially every attempt at building new computing platforms, and was the inspiration for a new effort at driving up the efficiency of processors running portable workloads. Virtualization was born from the need for servers – which would soon become the heart of data centers – to maximize the efficiency of their processors while driving the performance of their workloads.

In the early 2000s, engineers experimenting with the potential benefits of workload sharing among server processors came upon the realization that there may be a way to move active workloads between encapsulated virtual machines, from processor to processor – first along the same network fabric, and later across a much wider Internet pipeline than had ever been used before.

The major networking and virtualization products makers at the time were slow to embrace the concept. Live migration, some said, would be a passing fad. They believed as processors continued to gain performance advantages, workloads would become centralized, not distributed. But engineers at NASA proved them wrong, creating the first real-world use case for distributed processing across servers contained in shipping crates. They created the prototype for what we now call the cloud.

The 2010s – Big Power, Small Devices

The cloud can serve any class of device wherever it may reside, and however it may be connected. As a result, the old and oft-repeated promise of workplace mobility has finally become a reality. But it’s not the devices themselves that are responsible for mobility, but the servers and networks that support them.

The cloud can serve any class of device wherever it may reside, and however it may be connected. As a result, the old and oft-repeated promise of workplace mobility has finally become a reality. But it’s not the devices themselves that are responsible for mobility, but the servers and networks that support them.

Connectivity has become a commodity, as constant as electricity and running water, yet it follows you wherever you go. Compute power has become centralized once again, and the data center — once again designed by NASA — has regained its place at the heart of computing. These data centers, thanks to the hybrid cloud and the precedent set by Amazon, have evolved to become service providers. Virtualization now enables workloads to be staged in one data center, scaled up and down, and moved to other data centers across the globe, all without customers noticing a service disruption.

Users are no longer tied down to single computers, and their data is no longer bound to hard drives. And thus, the nature of software is transforming all over again.

Paul Allen

Inducted 1998

It was the holiday season of 1974. Two former classmates of Lakeside Elementary School were walking through a Seattle mall, probably looking for something other than literature. Paul, older by two years, happened across the January 1975 issue of Popular Electronics, whose kit of the month was a real computer with a microprocessor that readers could build and program at home.

Bill, the youngest, had always been goading Paul to form a company with him. They had tried a traffic data pattern analysis company together, but it failed. Paul considered a company that could build programs that people could run on this Altair kit computer. Rather than build one himself, he devised an Altair simulator on a DEC PDP-10, which Bill would use to make a BASIC interpreter. Later, Paul realized that with simulators, they could devise programs for more than one type of computer. Thus Paul Allen conceived the first genuine software firm independent from hardware manufacturers. Juxtaposing some words he gleaned from magazine articles, in 1975, he dubbed his firm “Micro-Soft.”

Steve Ballmer

Inducted 1997

In the summer of 1980, IBM was preparing to produce a personal computer, and it was doing a poor job of keeping it a secret. Most of the people who built operating systems knew and even liked one another. So when IBM first approached Microsoft about producing an OS for a PC, Bill Gates referred IBM to another company: DRI, whose chief, Gary Kildall, was producing CP/M.

IBM took Gates’ advice. But during an initial 1981 meeting with IBM for which Kildall failed to show up, his wife refused to sign a non-disclosure agreement for a PC whose existence most of the world actually knew. So the IBM negotiators returned to Microsoft, where newly hired business manager Steve Ballmer took the lead before Gates could send them elsewhere. With IBM looking to minimize costs, Ballmer negotiated a deal where Microsoft produced PC-DOS in exchange for royalties, and the right to license DOS to others. Microsoft had not yet even contributed code to PC-DOS; it had purchased QDOS outright from a former employee.

It was the first operating system platform, running software made for the IBM PC on other computers — something CP/M could not do. Ballmer’s deal gave rise to the modern software industry.

Dr. Craig Barrett

Inducted 2006

By March of 2000, the past several years of collective speculation over the perpetually rising value of the technology market, came to an abrupt halt. Intel had just made a massive investment in licensing rights to a high-speed memory technology from Rambus. This led to a price premium for PCs that competitor AMD exploited to its advantage, building chipsets for motherboards that allowed for less expensive DRAM chips.

After the “dot-com bubble” burst, and the terrorist strikes on New York and Washington, sales of PCs with Intel chips fell, and Intel stock plummeted. The company’s veteran COO Craig Barrett had been CEO for only two years, and was starting to be blamed for the downturn. Barrett’s bold response in 2001 began with a re-assessment of the strategies that led to the decision to partner with Rambus. That study found that too many architectural decisions had been made for separate components, without regard to how they affected the platform as a whole. Then, during the depths of the recession, Barrett ordered an $8 billion re-investment in a single, synchronized R&D track. It came to be known as the “tick-tock” strategy, and it powered Intel back into a position of market dominance.

Jim’s genuine approach to taking care of people and the business is what makes his story heartfelt. He never lost sight of why CompuCom was successful.

Frank Vitagliano

Inducted 2017

All careers – even the most successful ones – have to start somewhere, a sentiment 2017 IT Hall of Famer Frank Vitagliano echoes in reflecting on his 40-year career in IT. His beginnings in the industry were humble – starting in the mail room at IBM in 1977. Nevertheless, he applied himself to the role, vowing to, in his words, "show up and get things done."

This credo served him well, and over the next three decades he climbed upward through the ranks to end his tenure at Big Blue as its top channel executive – a long way from stuffing envelopes in mailboxes. The accolades came – in 2003, his work at IBM earned him the Lifetime Achievement Award from VARBusiness Magazine. In 2008, Vitagliano was inducted into the Ingram Micro VentureTech Network (VTN) Hall of Fame.

Upon retirement from IBM in 2005, Vitagliano moved to Juniper Networks as senior vice president and global channel chief, solidifying the vendor’s position as a channel-friendly networking and security leader. In 2013, Vitagliano joined Dell as vice president of global channel strategy and programs, growing its channel business, distribution sales and channel community engagement. Today, Vitagliano serves as CEO of Computex Technology Solutions.

Across his time at IBM, Juniper and Dell, Vitagliano has set the standard for partner engagement, helping both vendors and their channel partners realize their business goals and creating a stronger, more successful IT industry.

Carol Bartz

Inducted 2006

As late the early 1990s, not all software companies believed they had a mandate to be structured like corporations. In 1991, Alvar Green had found himself in a precarious position. He had been the executive vice president of finance for Autodesk for a very long time, and for emphasizing the bottom line, he had been relegated to the CEO’s post. Fed up with calling meetings that no one — even the chairman — would bother to attend, Green tendered his resignation. But before he did, he found perhaps the least likely possible successor: Carol Bartz, a respected vice president at Sun Microsystems, and a hard-liner businesswoman to the core.

One of Bartz’ first moves in 1992 was to cancel the weekly company drinking parties, called “beer busts.” That made her, for a time, less respected than her predecessor, until her subordinates watched her deal, day by day, with the process of shaping up her company to perform like a publicly traded corporation, all while battling breast cancer — and winning. She trimmed development projects, focusing less on the experimental and more on the commercially viable. Soon, Bartz was the unchallenged leader of the organization, holding the CEO post for 14 years.

Bell Laboratories

Inducted 1999

The programs that gave purpose to the first business computers were not very portable. When universities wanted to lease time on their computers to other institutions, the cost of clearing out one tenant’s programs and punching in the next one’s, actually made the timesharing concept unaffordable.

Inspired by a 1964 Atlantic Monthly article entitled, “The Computers of Tomorrow,” a consortium of the world’s computer laboratories investigated the feasibility of an “information utility,” similar to an electric or communications utility. The group needed automation to change out the programs, so together they developed a “control program” for the GE-645, called MULTICS. But consortium member Bell Labs concluded it was too unwieldy. In April 1969, Bell pulled out of the project to pursue its own alternative.

Priority #1 for Bell was to ensure the new system could run engineer Ken Thompson’s cool game, Space Travel. With that in mind, Thompson and partner Dennis Ritchie re-created the functionality of the GE-645 unit for a DEC PDP-7. By October, the new “operating system” was functional. Another year passed before they settled upon a good name for it: UNIX.

C. Gordon Bell

Inducted 2000

Through the 1960s, Digital Equipment Corporation did more than any other manufacturer to make computers smaller and less expensive. But it also sparked the transition of the computing device from a mere calculation engine into a data communications device.

This realization came about as a result of an architectural strategy of componentization — making smaller, interchangeable parts that fit together. DEC’s principal engineer, and the designer of its more successful Programmable Data Processor (PDP) series, was Gordon Bell. He accomplished componentization through the creation of the data bus, enabling integrated circuits to plug into a system and expand it. But communicating with the bus required synchronization with the clock of the machine running the bus. This made it hard to envision connecting any device with an independent processor.

So as essentially a means to an end, for the PDP-1 in 1960, Bell invented a component called a universal asynchronous receiver/transmitter (UART). Chips that receive data from asynchronous sources today, are still called UARTs. Transferring data through a UART required a specified set of parallel connections: an interface. What may have seemed at the time a side project, became the most important contributor to computing until the advent of the microchip.

Sir Tim Berners-Lee

Inducted 1998

By March 1989, an Apple Macintosh program called HyperCard was two years old, and being tested for a version 2.0 release. At the European nuclear research laboratory CERN, scientists and educators used Macs to write documents and draw diagrams. But all of CERN’s shared assets were stored on a DEC VAX mainframe with the VMS operating system, using a centralized management framework called CERNDOC.

Replacing CERNDOC with something that worked like a Mac word processor, would never be an option. So one of CERN’s physicists — a veteran of HyperCard, as well as a document database called Enquire and a game called simply “Adventure” — floated a document proposing that references to CERNDOC’s hierarchy could be embedded in the documents themselves. This way, selecting a reference would immediately bring up the document being referred to. Borrowing a term coined in 1963 by sociologist Ted Nelson — “hypertext” — Tim Berners-Lee humbly suggested, to anyone who was interested, that he could make document retrieval both faster and more efficient by decentralizing its indexing, and by making the server and client, or “browser,” separate programs. He described the interconnection between documents with the terms “mesh” and “web,” but the second one caught on.

D. James Bidzos

Inducted 2000

Throughout the 1960s, the essential business of the National Security Agency was encrypting communications. As long as computers remained the sacrosanct property of a privileged few companies, sensitive data could be protected simply by virtue of its own obscurity.

In 1982, the three creators of the RSA algorithm formed RSA Security — a commercial endeavor dedicated not to breaking the NSA, but rather ensuring the NSA did not break the public’s right to privacy. But RSA was soon portrayed as a counter-culture company, and even a threat to the government. For commercial cryptography to succeed as a product, it needed a product man. So RSA cryptographer Whitfield Diffie courted Paradyne veteran James Bidzos.

By 1986, Bidzos had been elevated to President and CEO of RSA. In 1994, when the U.S. Government began promoting encryption hardware called the “Clipper Chip” with a built-in back door for investigators, Bidzos took a strong stand against it. Yet rather than declaring the NSA the company’s enemy, he portrayed the agency as having declared war against fair commerce, in a public relations battle that RSA won — and surprisingly soon. Then in 1995, with RSA’s blessing, Bidzos founded the first commercial authentication certificate firm, Verisign.

Dave Boucher

Inducted 2000

For several years, a single American magazine — Computer Shopper — provided the principal point of contact for an entire segment of the PC industry: direct sales. But during the late 1990s, that fact changed as most of the magazine’s advertisers, led by Dell Computer, deployed working Web sites that presented users with interactive catalogs, and took orders directly from them, bypassing telephones and product codes. Dell had already revolutionized direct sales, and was planning to do so again.

By 1998, David Boucher had already spent 28 years at IBM, the previous 15 of which building the company’s sales and distribution channel. For IBM to reclaim the lead in PCs and forestall a Web effort that Michael Dell had already pre-announced, Boucher championed an Advanced Fulfillment Initiative (AFI) that, to be effective, would radically alter the design of the IBM chassis. Through AFI, customers would order IBM PCs online. The company’s existing channel of resellers would become regional manufacturers, assembling parts to order using a “Snap” model according to customers’ preferences.

There were mixed results, and some resellers complained about manufacturing and other costs being transferred to them from IBM. But the move arguably revived the PC brand.

Paul Brainerd

Inducted 1997

It was 1984, and Apple had just introduced Macintosh. Although the Apple II personal computer established the brand’s place in the office well before IBM, the Apple III flopped badly, and the Lisa was too expensive. So the Mac had an uphill battle ahead. To its disadvantage, it was underpowered and lacked software.

The year before, Paul Brainerd was working with a company called Atex, where he co-developed graphical terminals for pre-press editing and typesetting. But it had just been purchased by Kodak, along with his own stock in the company. With only a six-digit sum for capital, Brainerd figured the way to raise capital quickly would be to sell VCs on a kind of “convergence” idea that brought together the Mac’s graphics and Apple’s LaserWriter printer in an office setting. His theme was “desktop publishing.”

And Brainerd was rejected, so he approached some Apple employees — not the company directly — who introduced him to Adobe’s John Warnock, the creator of PostScript. With the employees’ help, he demonstrated to Apple a prototype of PageMaker, which used Atex methods to typeset PostScript for Macs. It was the Mac’s first “killer app,” and it did not come from Apple.

Dan Bricklin

Inducted 1998

He wanted to build a calculating device, with a screen that projected a grid, and a shiny, red ball that directed the pointer on that grid. In the meantime, Dan Bricklin thought, he’d prototype the idea of the program on one of those microcomputers that he saw folks at Harvard Business School installing on their TVs. The finance professor there pointed Bricklin to a student named Dan Fylstra, who loaned him an Apple II for a weekend.

One weekend in 1978 was all he needed to produce a demo — albeit slow — of a program where arithmetic functions were embedded in cells in a grid that carried numbers. The name “spreadsheet” probably sprouted forth organically, like a happy accident. What it would become was the first modern application. With coding partner and fellow Harvard student Bob Frankston, Bricklin would produce the first modern computer application — the first program designed to wait for input, receive it at the user’s discretion, and react with results. By 1979, VisiCalc would become the premiere product of Fylstra’s company, Personal Software — not Apple or Radio Shack or Commodore, but an independent firm. The software industry started here.

John Seely Brown

Inducted 2004

For a good part of the 20th century, a significant share of the world’s research in human interaction with technology was conducted by Xerox. Much of it led to the publication of reports. Some of it, like the Palo Alto Research Center’s studies of the sociology of workplaces, produced the graphical computing concepts that Apple copied for its Macintosh.

But some 17 years after he proposed PARC to Xerox, with the promise that it would not produce business value in five years’ time but it certainly would in ten, Xerox Chief Scientist John Seely Brown produced something of true value. It was a concept for computer-based diagnosis of systems based on his team’s observation of the learning patterns of elementary school kids.

In 1986, Brown’s BUGGY project conducted analytics on the errors these kids were making on math quizzes. It concluded there were patterns behind these errors, and that it was possible to identify their sources. Today, Brown’s research is being applied to new endeavors in machine learning, applying a remedial approach to isolating the factors that lead systems to draw inaccurate conclusions.

Rod Canion

Inducted 1997

The first non-IBM PCs capable of running MS-DOS and all of its associated software were generally known as “PC clones.” In the winter of 1981, a handful of companies — some established, some startups — came separately to the conclusion that it was theoretically possible to engineer a computer capable of running MS-DOS without copying IBM’s intellectual property. Of course, IBM was likely to challenge them anyway, but if they were legally in the right, perhaps they could afford to weather the storm.

Rod Canion didn’t want simply to produce another PC clone, as Columbia Data products and Texas Instruments — his old employer — were already doing. In January 1982, as two other fellow TI compatriots, Jim Harris and Bill Murto, joined Canion in sketching their ideas on the back of a restaurant place mat, before long their sketch included a handle. They decided to make a PC that was smaller, more portable, yet more durable than an IBM PC, while at the same time providing a customer commitment to 100% compatibility.

What developed from this discussion was the genus for a portable computer that justified its premium price tag — over $3,500. Together, they introduced the first Compaq Portable in November 1982.

Vinton G. “Vint” Cerf

Inducted 1998

At a hotel conference area in Washington, D.C., on October 24, 1972, the consulting firm of Bolt Baranek and Newman had pulled off a brilliant demonstration of packet switching using (by most eyewitness accounts) three computers. Assisting BBN as principal programmer was a 29-year old with a freshly minted Ph.D. from UCLA, on loan from IBM, named Vint Cerf.

Programmers like to think aloud, but good programmers speak softly. It was during this conference that Cerf was overheard softly making overtures about the concept of devices that could connect networks. He called these in-between points “interconnects,” and he was overheard having dubbed the network that bridged these points an “internet.”

That day, the people most interested in this idea formed an official organization: the International Packet Network Working Group. Without objection, Cerf was immediately elected its chairman. The following June, Cerf led an international seminar, from which he and his fellow participants would produce A Protocol for Packet Network Intercommunication, published by the IEEE in May 1974. It established the prototype for Transport Control Protocol. Today, TCP is the essential instruction manual for how the Internet works, and will always work.

John Chambers

It was December 1998. Don Listwin, an executive vice president at Cisco Systems, chaired a company presentation. Among those present were the vice president of marketing and the CEO, John Chambers, in his third year at the helm. Still the new man, Chambers was already on record as projecting a total network industry convergence around Internet Protocol, at the heart of Cisco’s router production business.

Listwin’s presentation included several references to the phrase “new world.” Eyewitnesses say Chambers not only adopted the phrase immediately, but began referring to technologies and competitor vendors belonging to the “old world.” It was here that Chambers would begin the process of assembling a “New Internet Ecosystem,” based not just on Cisco’s existing pattern of acquisitions, but partnering with well-known suppliers and technology providers.

Just two months later, Cisco and Motorola would launch the ecosystem in a joint announcement. Though their partnership lasted little more than a year, the launch quickly built momentum for Cisco, vanquishing competitors Nortel Networks and Cabletron, and initiating the process that sublimated the Lucent brand into an acquisition of an acquisition. That these competitors would cease to exist was a prediction made almost solely by Chambers.

Jim Clark

Inducted 2004

In the summer of 1979, a team of engineers who built hardware prototypes for Xerox’ PARC research center, gave an extended demonstration of how to custom-build semiconductors, for some of the faculty of Stanford University. One of them was Lynn Conway, who had co-created the Mead-Conway method for Very Large Scale Integration (VLSI). In the room was a researcher and professor named James H. Clark, who was immediately taken with Conway’s method.

Within months, Clark would write and publish articles about his epiphany. VLSI, he believed, would be an inexpensive way of producing very fast parallelism — executing sequences of instructions over very large spans of input data. Only through parallelism, he demonstrated, could computers render detailed graphics in real-time to be used for real-world situations, such as architectural modeling. Clark was inspired by how VLSI was already being put to use producing arithmetic logic units (ALUs) for smaller and smaller computers.

Obviously Clark invented the graphics processor as we know it today. But that invention would be a patent on a shelf if he had not also founded in 1982 the business that first produced the thing. Invoking a title that explained itself, he dubbed his venture Silicon Graphics.

Edgar F. “Ted” Codd

Inducted 2004

It was the sort of paper whose subject matter was enough to convey the identity of its publisher: IBM. Its subject was the growing problem of maintaining continuity among associated elements of data. In the 1950s, that task was simple: You made certain all the punch cards were in the right box. By 1969, magnetic media was taking over, and data had to be kept organized in software.

F. Codd, Research Division, San Jose, California.” Codd would perfect this “view” the following year, for a 1970 paper for the ACM that presented the “Relational Model.” There, he introduced the computing world to two mathematical concepts that had, heretofore, not applied to it: first-order predicate logic, and the theory of relations. A relation, as he explained it, was an unordered set of tuples to which organization may be applied later. Inducted 1998

Toward the end of the 1980s, PCs were running out of memory. A byte was only 16 binary digits wide, and both data and address busses were only one byte wide, limiting addressable memory to the breadth of the memory map. The solution, PC enthusiasts knew, would be a 32-bit address bus. But IBM wanted to control the 32-bit market, as well as the hardware that attached to it. So while IBM produced its first PS/2 computers with Micro Channel Architecture (MCA), Compaq competed with a completely different 32-bit architecture called EISA, that it shared with other manufacturers in hopes of building a standard.

At first, Intel treated this as a win/win for itself. But with MCA carrying stiff royalties, and EISA requiring new manufacturing techniques, 32-bit PCs became too expensive, and customers were sticking with their 286-based AT-compatibles. So Intel’s Andrew Grove put engineer Dennis Carter in charge of producing a stopgap solution. In 1988, Carter’s team produced the 386SX processor with a 32-bit data bus, but a 16-bit address bus compatible with existing AT architectures. Not only would Carter’s 386SX sell millions of units, but the entire PC market surged, effectively terminating further production for both 32-bit architectures.

Phil Katz

Inducted 2011

Not all great accomplishments in computing took place in a spirit of glory, good cheer, and celebration. PKWare Inc. became a profitable concern because one man, Phil Katz (the “PK” in the title), was a particularly effective programmer of a tool that the emerging online user desperately needed: a mathematical compressor for software packages distributed online.

Compressing the number of bytes consumed by a file is usually accomplished with an algorithm that replaces repeated sequences of bytes with symbols that are shorter than the sequences themselves. The first popular algorithm for that purpose was ARC, and Katz produced a machine language version for PCs that ran blazingly fast. But in an era before open source was a viable option for commercial coders, the ARC formula had been patented, and Katz was sued. He responded by switching formulas, though Lempel-Ziv (the latter syllable having been referred to colloquially as “Zip”) was also patented. Katz’ PKZIP was even more popular, but after a new round of legal battles, he ended up developing his own “ZIP” compression format in 1989. Katz was one of the most successful self-made businessmen in computing, until a competitor ironically appropriated his format in 1991.

Inducted 1999

The first PC networks, well before the phrase “local-area network” was coined, made the images of disk drives available to multiple units simultaneously. That’s all that disk servers did. When two copies of an application read and wrote records to the same database, it was up to the application to sort out which input/output operation was “legitimate” and which would be tossed out.

In 1981, Drew Major staked his claim as the principal architect of Novell’s first file server, to be called NetWare. His first rendition was a disk server for CP/M machines, and he immediately saw the drawbacks. He developed a “star topology” for a single computer that arbitrated networking requests from multiple clients. That computer would maintain its own, native operating system, its own database, and a network file system (NFS) that was independent from the clients. It was the first client/server model network orchestration of any kind — the innovation that finally convinced large enterprises to migrate their assets away from mainframes. Novell NetWare commanded the network OS market for the next decade-and-a-half — not even Microsoft could break through. Along with colleagues Kyle Powell and Dale Neibaur, Major led the effort to create NetWare.

University of California Berkeley Ninja Project

Inducted 2000

It was the spring of 1997. A group of UC Berkeley students and faculty, led by then-Assistant Professor of Computer Science Eric Brewer, undertook a challenge that might, or might not, bear fruit. It was the idea that the Web could serve as a transport not just for pages but for services. If everyday services were made basic, “atomic” (indivisible and, where necessary, reversible) and standardized, the UC Berkeley team concluded, there should be no reason why these services couldn’t be provided for any device, anywhere, regardless of size or location.

From the very beginning of their work, the UC Berkeley team was absolutely correct about two premier necessities of scalable architecture. One was that each client in the process should be capable of scaling the input and output channels to suit its own capabilities. Secondly, the servers in the process should by capable of dynamically determining the best way to fulfill each service request, at the time they receive it. That dynamic path came to be known as the “ninja path;” and thus, the team’s work was soon dubbed the Ninja Project.

Univ. of Illinois U-C National Center for Supercomputing Applications

Inducted 2000

During the 1990s, the meaning of “supercomputing” was changing. The National Science Foundation was funding the National Center for Supercomputing Applications at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign since 1986. The emphasis there was on applications, not machines. It was becoming too expensive for NCSA to continue running its Cray supercomputers. Some at the University were actively considering whether supercomputers were necessary in order to build supercomputer applications.

While that deliberation was ongoing, in 1993, two students named Marc Andreessen and Eric Bina spent a mere three months on an implementation of a client program for the emerging World-Wide Web. Their mission was to merge networked hypertext, which Tim Berners-Lee had already demonstrated, with multimedia. There were already Web browsers being actively distributed, so Andreessen and Bina did not need to reinvent the wheel. What they needed was an application that upheld the ideals that supercomputing used to uphold, but without the supercomputers. By leveraging the power of millions of Web servers, NCSA Mosaic – the first multimedia-enabled Web browser – hit upon the most upscale application there has ever been. For a year, Mosaic succeeded under NCSA’s stewardship. Its practitioners would leave to found Netscape in 1994.

University of Washington HIT Lab

Inducted 2000

Virtual Reality Exposure Therapy (VRET) is a concept that emerged from the work of researchers at the Human Interface Technology Laboratory (HITL) at the University of Washington. HITL was founded in 1989 as a proving ground for experiments in how to apply virtual reality to applications that directly benefit society. Not that gaming technology is somehow a waste of effort, but the artistry behind VR may also be applicable to improving lives, and even restoring them.

Six years after HITL’s founding, three of its key researchers – Ari Hollander, Kimberley Osberg Lippman, and Howard Rose. Their commercial enterprise, Firsthand Technology, emerged from their work. In one of the first culminations of their work to be tried outside the laboratory, they modeled a virtual “snow world” to be shown through VR glasses or projected using a device called the Wheatstone Stereoscope, to patients suffering from the pain of burn related injuries. And in the aftermath of the terrorist attack on New York City on September 11, 2001, Firsthand was among the researchers experimenting with projecting realistic models of an unscarred New York City, to help victims of post-traumatic stress disorder ease their way back into a normal environment.

Roy Vallee

Inducted 2007

When the Web first started taking hold as the method of choice for ordering equipment, parts, and services, it was expected to be a simple replacement for the printed catalog and the telephone line. What suppliers and distributors in the channel did not anticipate — for some, until it was too late — was that the Web would network their customers directly with their supply chains, completely rewriting the laws of supply and demand.

In 1998, Avnet — an electronics equipment supplier since 1955 — named Roy Vallee to its CEO post. Immediately upon taking command, Avnet transitioned to a logistics company, making several acquisitions in the process. It began offering a piece of software entitled, curiously enough, Rapid Benefits Estimation Tool (RaBET). Its purpose was to gauge the fluctuations in inventory levels, and effectively chart a visible course for customers for outsourcing their supply chain management, based on their own seasonal demand levels. RaBET introduced Avnet’s customers to principles of economics that were being hard-wired onto the global economy by the Web. Vallee’s retooling of Avnet charted, quite literally, the path for it to become IBM’s largest channel partner in North America.

VentureTech Network Partners

Inducted 2012

At the turn of the century, electronics and computer superstores such as CompUSA were, willingly or not, choking the lifeblood of small resellers throughout the U.S. and Canada. Although Ingram was the largest distributor to these SMBs, it could not afford to keep losing too many of them to low-margin, high-volume competitors.

Ted Warner was one of the founders of Connecting Point of Greeley, Colorado. His was one of the first computer store brands to consolidate into a nationwide franchise during the 1980s. As the 2000s were approaching, there were fewer conferences to enable vendors to share expertise and ideas for adapting to a changing economy. So Warner helped to build an industry forum. Together with Myles Schacter, a reseller in Lawrence, Kansas; Steven Harper, the president of Network Management Group, Hutchinson, Kansas; and Jane Cage, the COO of Heartland Technology Solutions in Joplin, Missouri, he formed an industry forum with the unassuming name of Business Technology Centers.

In 1998, when Ingram was studying how to bring vendors together as partners in an industry forum, it found there already was one. It purchased BTC, renamed the group VentureTech Network, and assigned its SMB vice president, Rick Sbrocca, to lead it.

Charles Wang

Inducted 1998

History will paint a portrait of Charles Wang, the founder of Computer Associates — which we know today as CA Technologies — with a variety of brushes and an unusual assortment of colors. The brighter shades of this portrait were first supplied in 1969 by — perhaps ironically now — the U.S. Dept. of Justice, when it filed its anti-monopoly suit against IBM.

It was a case with no end, and would be dropped in 1982. But in the meantime, during those critical years when IBM’s secret entry into the PC was being developed and rumors were being propagated, IBM made a public demonstration of unbundling its software from its smaller computers. In 1976, Wang and his business partner, Russell Artzt, capitalized. With every cent of capital they could borrow, they established a service bureau in New York that would give Swiss software firm Computer Associates an entry into the US market. CA had a program based on little more than a sorting algorithm, called CA-Sort. Wang’s move gave CA the opportunity it needed to exploit the U.S. market, as a kind of organizational database for IBM’s own mainframe software. CA-Sort’s success launched Wang’s initiative to acquire the parent company in 1980.

John Warnock

Inducted 1998

It was 1974, and the U.S. Maritime Academy had commissioned the graphics research firm Evans & Sutherland to produce a real-time simulation of New York Harbor, as seen from the bridge of a ship passing through. The job required a massive database of 3D geometry, and the engineers there decided it made little sense to use a database that was originally engineered for any other purpose than 3D geometry. So they created a written language parser that interpreted this geometry in a manner its engineers could readily understand. One of the associates in the room was John Warnock.

He took his lessons with him to Xerox PARC in 1978. When Warnock had the opportunity to build a graphics interpreter around PARC’s AI language LISP, he made a decision that was part-selfish and part-prescient: Using M&A’s framework to produce a language for which he could get some credit, he produced a general-purpose printing instruction protocol called Interpress. Yet Xerox was always a hardware company first, so it declined to exploit Interpress further than for its own laser printers. Sensing Xerox’ short-sightedness, in 1982, Warnock and Charles Geschke left PARC, taking Interpress with them to produce PostScript, the foundation of Adobe Systems.

Steve Wozniak

Inducted 1998

Some day, engineers will make it possible for the world to work four days a week instead of five, Steve Wozniak’s dad told him when he was little. Washing machines, he said, freed people from the tedium of their lives, and in so doing, changed the world. So Wozniak declared himself an engineer.

If you reduce the list of things engineers could do to or for the Apple II to a manageable handful, Steve Jobs argued one day in 1977, then the company would have greater opportunity to capitalize on expandability for itself. Woz, who was designing the Apple II at the time, did not disagree, though he didn’t like the implication. Possibilities, Woz argued, are what people see in a computer. The Apple I had been expandable, in the same sense that any open book could receive scribbles in the margins. Jobs wanted two expansion slots, and only two. But Woz had been conferring with some colleagues about a radically improved design for an expansion bus. He foresaw eight expansion slots on a bus controlled by only two chips. Woz built what he intended to build anyway. His stubbornness and his brilliance had combined to change the world.

Xerox Palo Alto Research Center

Inducted 1998

A single photograph of the original Alto computer, without any accompanying text whatsoever, may give its viewer a clear impression that its producer had invented the personal computer. Alto had all the concepts we now consider critical to computing, in their primordial form: an interactive keyboard, a high-resolution display, a mouse that directed a pointer to graphics on the screen, integrated storage, and perhaps most importantly of all, the first Ethernet network.

The basic concept for Alto formed like the assembly of a solar system, in the mind of Robert W. Taylor. In 1970, Taylor began assembling the Computer Science Laboratory department of Xerox’ Palo Alto Research Center. Typically, when a research group forms, it’s the researchers who have all the brilliant ideas and the department head who needs to be convinced. This time, it was the reverse. During this time, PARC engineers Butler Lampson and Robert Metcalfe were among those contributing to the MAXC concept. During a 1972 retreat, Taylor tried convincing them to build MAXC with a bitmapped display, but as Lampson recalls, Taylor failed to convince them... at first. Over the following months, Taylor’s vision compelled them to build the first working Alto model in 1973.

Philip Zimmermann

Inducted 2001

It was called the Comprehensive Counter-Terrorism Act of 1991. Its principal sponsor was a senator from Delaware just beginning his fourth term, named Joseph R. Biden, Jr. Title II, Subtitle B, was very vaguely worded. “It is the sense of Congress,” it began, which on the surface sounded like Congress was rendering an opinion over drinks at a dinner party. Loose words could be loosely interpreted. And it gave lawmakers the impression that they could mandate computer security tools be equipped with back-doors for government investigators.

If privacy is outlawed, believed Phil Zimmermann, then only outlaws would have privacy. Zimmermann, who ran a small software producer, had a computer science degree, yet he loathed calculus. Angered by what he saw happening in the Senate, he found himself approached by Charles Merritt, an Arkansas cryptographer who had earlier developed an implementation of RSA’s public-key algorithm for Z80-based CP/M systems. If encryption were to have been intentionally crafted to be weaker, Zimmermann believed, then anyone or anything could exploit that weakness, not just governments. He used Merritt’s algorithms to help him overcome his own personal weak links in math, to build an encryption system he called Pretty Good Privacy.

Richard Inatome

Inducted 1999

Throughout the 1970s, the major computer manufacturers did not regard the embryonic personal computer business as an emerging threat to their industry until the revenue chain started taking shape. Boutique stores were the first retailers to establish the brand presence of Apple, Commodore, Atari, and the brands that have since faded into history: Amstrad, Ohio Scientific, Exidy, and Apricot. Before many of these stores consolidated into chains, they began sharing names, perhaps unintentionally. There were “Computer Mart” stores across America and Canada before there was a Computer Mart chain.

In 1976, one of those stores named Computer Mart in Royal Oak, Michigan was being run by Joseph Inatome, along with his son Rick as manager. They saw the advantage of name recognition, so they began acquiring and consolidating as many as 15 Computer Mart and Computer City stores, collectively under Rick’s leadership. Studying the success of his group’s advertising, Rick realized that consumers invested their trust in stores before they’d invest it in brands. He began a cooperative advertising scheme, which soon expanded into a big part of the burgeoning PC sales channel — a part that soon took on the name Inacom Corp.

Steve Raymund

Inducted 1999

The perfect situation for a salesperson to find himself in, is the indispensable middleman between supplier and consumer. In 1974, Ed Raymund put himself in a very interesting situation. His day job was as a sales representative for major electronics manufacturers. His customers needed not just minicomputers and office machines, but the everyday supplies that go with them — rolls of paper, gear oil, black and red ink ribbons. So Ed formed a supply operation on the side, doing business as Tech Data.

By 1981, the side business had become a headache for Ed. He had hired a management team to run it for him, but they wanted to buy him out. Ed wanted to keep Tech Data in the family, so he sold it to his son Steve for the price of an automobile. The management team immediately left, taking their accounts with them. Rather than start all over and cultivate a new set, Steve took a gamble that Tech Data could make more money distributing office supplies in bulk than selling them to customers by the box. By 1986, when he assumed the CEO role, Steve Raymund had built Tech Data into one of the nation’s largest computer distributors.
